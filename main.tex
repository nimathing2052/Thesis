\begin{abstract}
Nepal’s 2022 elections marked a turning point in political communication, with social media platforms such as Facebook, YouTube, and TikTok emerging as powerful arenas for political engagement amid widespread youth disillusionment with traditional parties. Beyond platform adoption, virality on TikTok—measured through likes, shares, and comments—became an important part of how political narratives circulated and gained visibility online.

Despite TikTok’s growing influence, little research has systematically examined how political content gains visibility on the platform, particularly in low-income, multilingual democracies such as Nepal. Understanding virality in this context is methodologically innovative—leveraging multimodal machine learning on audio, image, and text data—and substantively important for explaining how digital platforms reshape electoral dynamics.

This study analyzes 2,964 political TikTok videos accessed via TikTok’s Research API, applying machine learning and regression-based association analysis to examine patterns of engagement. A leakage-safe virality prediction pipeline shows that political virality is moderately predictable from pre-upload information, but that sender characteristics dominate performance. Across alternative target definitions, models that include sender variables outperform content-only and platform-only setups, indicating that structural creator capacity matters more than multimodal content features alone.

For content-level analysis, regression results show that videos associated with independent political actors achieve higher engagement on average than general election-themed or legacy-party content, even after adjusting for follower reach. By contrast, communication-style coefficients are unstable across model specifications and diagnostic checks. Propensity-score and within-creator diagnostics indicate that styles such as Charisma/Leadership are highly endogenous to specific actors, limiting causal interpretation and suggesting that styles operate as structural signals rather than transferable tactics.

Taken together, the findings support a framework in which political virality on TikTok is shaped primarily by sender capacity, secondarily by actor-linked content signaling, and only indirectly by platform affordances observed through metadata. The study contributes an open-source multimodal analysis pipeline, an explicit discussion of operationalization tradeoffs, and a clearer framework for studying political virality on short-video platforms without overstating causal claims. In plain terms, the policy lesson is simple: social media can widen access to political information and help newer actors gain attention, but election governance on short-video platforms should focus not only on harmful content, but also on who gets seen, how that visibility can be checked, and whether public institutions in Nepal can independently audit political reach.
\end{abstract}

\chapter{Introduction}
Nepal's 2022 local elections (2079 B.S.) marked a visible shift in political communication, with TikTok joining Facebook and YouTube as an important campaign arena \parencite{nepalitimes2025}. Political actors used these platforms to circulate speeches, campaign clips, satire, music, and commentary, especially among younger voters who were increasingly frustrated with legacy parties \parencite{FPExplainers2024, dahal2023influence}. In this sense, election communication was no longer shaped only by party organizations, news outlets, and rallies. It was also shaped by short-video platforms and the creators who could attract attention on them.

This shift mattered especially for independent candidates. Balendra Shah (Balen Shah), for example, became highly visible online through a mix of campaign symbolism, youth appeal, and creator support \parencite{DW2022NepalElections}. The ``lauro'' (stick) hashtag became a recognizable symbol of the independent movement \parencite{AnnapurnaExpress2022}, and influential creators such as ``Routine of Nepal Banda'' helped increase the visibility of newer political actors \parencite{dahal2023influence}. Balen Shah ultimately won the Kathmandu mayoral race with 38.6\% of the vote \parencite{wikipedia2022local}. Legacy parties remained dominant overall, but the campaign showed that newer actors could use social media to gain public attention outside traditional party and media structures.
\newpage
TikTok matters analytically because it can circulate content beyond a creator's immediate follower network and because it makes remix, sound, and visual performance central to political communication \parencite{guinaudeau2022fifteen}. At the same time, the platform's internal ranking system is not directly observable to researchers. This means that TikTok is important to study, but it must be studied carefully: we can observe outcomes and metadata, but not the platform's full recommendation process. In Nepal, this matters even more because TikTok became politically controversial after it was banned in 2023 and reinstated in 2024 amid debates about misinformation, governance, and digital regulation \parencite{lamichhane2024tik, reuters2024tiktok}.

Despite growing API access \parencite{tiktokResearchAPI2025}, little research has examined political virality on TikTok in Nepal through a multimodal lens. This thesis addresses that gap by asking two simpler questions. First, how well can virality be predicted from pre-upload sender, content, and platform features? Second, how are communication styles and political content themes associated with virality? The goal is not to infer TikTok's hidden algorithm, but to explain observable patterns of political attention during an election period.

This study offers four key contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Academic}: It examines a substantively important but under-studied case of election-related TikTok communication in Nepal, contributing evidence from a multilingual Global South context.
    \item \textbf{Methodological}: It develops and evaluates a multimodal pipeline for virality prediction while explicitly testing how results change when the virality target is defined in more or less view-dependent ways.
\newpage
    \item \textbf{Conceptual}: It proposes a framework in which political virality is shaped by sender capacity, content signaling, and platform affordances, while emphasizing that observed style effects are actor-bound associations rather than proven causal strategies.
    \item \textbf{Policy-facing}: It suggests that, in Nepal, the Election Commission and other digital-policy actors should protect the civic benefits of social media while improving transparency of content distribution, research access to election-period data, and recognition of digital reach as a politically relevant campaign resource.
\end{itemize}

This research lays the groundwork for future cross-platform virality studies (e.g., TikTok, YouTube Shorts) and for more tightly identified work on political visibility in low-income electoral contexts.
\newpage

\chapter{Literature Review}
\subsection{Social Media, Political Attention, and Algorithmic Gatekeeping}

Social media has changed how political attention is distributed. Earlier agenda-setting research focused on editors, journalists, and party elites as the main gatekeepers of public visibility. On digital platforms, ranking systems and user engagement now play a larger role in deciding which messages travel widely and which do not.

Evidence from \parencite{Gilardi02012022} suggests that digital platforms have changed agenda-setting power without eliminating older actors entirely. Gilardi et al. show that media agendas, party agendas, and politician agendas now shape one another rather than following a single hierarchy. For this thesis, the key point is simpler: visibility is increasingly shaped by platform systems as well as by political actors themselves.

In Nepal, social media has become an important space for political expression, especially among younger citizens who are dissatisfied with legacy parties and traditional media \parencite{dahal2023influence, bhandari2024}. Yet the mechanisms behind online political visibility remain underexplained. Existing work is often descriptive, leaving open the question of why some political content receives much more attention than other content during elections.

\subsection{Conceptualizing Virality as Engagement-Weighted Political Attention}
``Virality'' is widely used but not always clearly defined. \parencite{berger2012viral} note that the term is often used loosely to refer either to reach or to speed of diffusion. On platforms such as TikTok and YouTube, many studies reduce virality to view counts alone \parencite{nissenbaum2017internet}. That is convenient, but it also mixes together two different things: how much a video circulated and how much baseline visibility the sender already had.

Recent computational work argues that virality should also reflect active engagement, not just passive exposure. \parencite{sah2025decoding} treat comments, shares, and reposts as stronger signals of attention than views alone. This matters in politics, where provocative or emotionally resonant content may generate strong reactions even when it does not come from the largest accounts.

Following this literature, the thesis treats political virality on TikTok as an engagement-weighted attention outcome. The goal is not to claim that this is the only valid measure of virality, but to use a measure that captures both visibility and user response while reducing reliance on raw view counts alone.

\subsection{Affective Information Behavior and Political Engagement in Nepal}
Research in political psychology increasingly treats emotion as part of political reasoning rather than as its opposite. This is relevant to TikTok because short-video platforms reward attention, reaction, and rapid interpretation.
\newpage
Affective Intelligence Theory (AIT) offers one useful way to think about this relationship \parencite{marcus2013affective}. In simple terms, the theory argues that different emotions push people toward different kinds of political processing. Enthusiasm can reinforce existing attachments, while anxiety can interrupt routine thinking and make people pay closer attention to new information. Anger, by contrast, can deepen existing loyalties and sharpen group boundaries. For this thesis, the value of AIT is not that it proves a platform effect, but that it helps explain why emotional tone may matter for political attention online.

This perspective also fits recent work from Nepal. \parencite{gurungemoji} analyzed Facebook emoji reactions during the 2022 Kathmandu mayoral race and found that emotional engagement varied strongly across candidates. Independent candidate Balen Shah drew unusually strong positive engagement, while establishment candidates more often attracted negative or mocking reactions. The result does not prove that online emotion determines electoral outcomes, but it does suggest that digital engagement can provide one useful signal of political attention and sentiment during campaigns.


\subsection{Platform Logic, Affordances, and Content Amplification}
Political visibility online is also shaped by platform affordances: the design choices and ranking systems that affect what users see and engage with. As \parencite{naranjo2025tweets} note, these affordances are not fully deterministic, but they do shape the conditions under which attention is distributed.

\textbf{Interest graph vs. social graph}: Unlike follower-centered platforms, TikTok can recommend content beyond an account's immediate network \parencite{theshelf2025, buffer2025}. This is one reason why it matters politically: some content may travel widely even when it does not come from the largest accounts.

\textbf{Emotional content and engagement}: Prior research suggests that engagement-based systems often reward emotionally charged content. \parencite{brady2017emotion} show that moral-emotional language on Twitter increased retweet rates, while \parencite{milli2025engagement} find that engagement-based ranking can amplify anger, sadness, and anxiety in political content.
\newpage
\textbf{Feedback dynamics}: When emotionally charged content receives stronger reactions, those reactions can become further visibility signals. This does not mean every emotional message goes viral, but it does suggest a plausible mechanism through which emotional and confrontational political content can receive disproportionate attention.


\subsection{Integrating the Framework: From Theory to Modeling Decisions}
Taken together, this literature suggests a simple way to think about virality in Nepal's 2022 election period. Virality is better understood as platform-mediated political attention than as a direct measure of persuasion or electoral impact.

Three ideas matter most for this thesis. First, \textbf{sender capacity} matters because videos do not enter TikTok on equal footing. Prior audience size, creator history, and account credibility shape how much attention a video can attract from the start. Second, \textbf{content signaling} matters because political videos carry style, identity, and emotional tone, not just information. Third, \textbf{platform affordances} matter because timing, ranking, remix features, and recommendation systems shape the environment in which sender and content signals are received.

The framework is intentionally modest. Sender and content factors can be observed directly in the data, while platform dynamics are only partly visible through metadata. This is why RQ1 focuses on prediction from observable pre-upload features, and why RQ2 is limited to conditional associations rather than causal claims about communication style.

\chapter{Conceptual framework}
This study treats political virality on TikTok as a form of platform-mediated political attention shaped by three elements: sender capacity, content signaling, and platform affordances (Figure~\ref{fig:conceptual_framework}). Rather than reducing virality to reach or view counts alone, the framework emphasizes that visibility and engagement are shaped by both structural and contextual factors. Platform dynamics are included, but only indirectly, because the platform's internal recommendation process is not observable in the available data.

Sender capacity captures the structural advantages that shape how content enters the platform environment. Follower count, prior creator engagement, verification status, and account tenure are treated as core predictors and controls because they proxy baseline audience reach, creator prominence, and accumulated platform familiarity. In this framework, sender capacity is expected to explain an important share of virality even before multimodal content features are considered.

Content signaling refers to how political messages are framed and performed, including communication styles (e.g., Charisma/Leadership, Comedic, Critique/Frustration) and political content themes (e.g., independent candidates versus legacy parties). In this thesis, styles are treated not as freely transferable tactics but as signals that may reflect identity, authenticity, and emotional orientation. Their association with engagement is therefore expected to depend partly on who is speaking and in what political context.

Platform affordances encompass TikTok's ranking environment and temporal dynamics, such as posting time and proximity to election day. Because the platform's internal recommendation process is unobserved, this study does not identify algorithmic effects directly. Instead, platform affordances are approximated through observable metadata and treated as part of the context within which sender and content factors operate. Within this framework, virality is defined as engagement-weighted political attention rather than raw popularity.

Together, these components inform the study's modeling strategy. The composite virality index reflects engagement-based attention; sender and timing variables capture structural advantages; and style and theme variables describe how political content is performed. The framework also clarifies the limits of causal inference: because communication styles are tied to specific actors and do not have strong counterfactual overlap in this dataset, their coefficients are interpreted as conditional associations rather than causal treatments.

\chapter{Research Design}
This study employs a mixed-methods approach, combining machine learning for virality prediction (RQ1) and regression-based content analysis for style–theme interactions (RQ2), to examine TikTok’s role in Nepal’s 2022 local elections. The design leverages multimodal data (text, audio, visual) and platform metadata, integrating computational techniques with political communication theory.

\section{Data Sources and Collection Strategy}
The dataset comprises 28,165 TikTok videos collected from March 15, 2022 (one month before candidacy registration) to May 13, 2022 (election day), capturing the campaign period of Nepal’s 2022 local elections. A hybrid approach was used, combining the Official TikTok Research API, the Unofficial TikTok-Api, and a custom subclass of TikTok-Content-Scraper \parencite{QBukold2025TikTokScraper}. The Official API collected video metadata (e.g., views, likes, shares, hashtags, descriptions) and user metadata (e.g., follower count, verified status) but was limited by quotas (1,000 users/day, 100,000 videos/day). The Unofficial API supplemented user-level data, while the custom scraper captured platform-specific features like \texttt{video\_diversification} labels, duet/stitch behavior, and music metadata.

Videos were collected via hashtag-based searches (e.g., \texttt{\#NepalElection2022}, \texthindi{\#चुनाव}, \texttt{\#LocalElection2022}), adapted from \parencite{pinto2024} (see Appendix~\ref{appendix:keywords}). After removing duplicates and irrelevant content, the final dataset included 28,165 videos. Initial features collected include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Platform/Engagement Metrics}: Views, likes, shares, comments.
    \item \textbf{User Features}: Follower count, video count, verified status.
    \item \textbf{Content Metadata}: Hashtags, video descriptions, audio metadata.
    \item \textbf{Temporal Features}: Posting timestamps (UTC).
\end{itemize}
The data collection pipeline and schema are illustrated in Figures~\ref{fig:data_pipeline} and~\ref{fig:data_schema}, respectively.

\subsection{Sampling}
For multimodal analysis, a stratified sample of 3,000 videos was drawn from the 28,165 videos based on view count percentiles:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High-Virality}: $\geq$ 95th percentile ($\geq$ 8,000 views; $\sim$5\% of dataset).
    \item \textbf{Medium-Virality}: 60th to 95th percentile ($\sim$35\% of dataset).
    \item \textbf{Low-Virality}: $\leq$ 60th percentile ($\sim$60\% of dataset).
\end{itemize}
After removing videos that became private or irrelevant, the final sample was 2,964 videos.

\section{Feature Engineering}
\subsection{Operationalizing Virality (Dependent Variable)}
While there is no universally accepted standard for measuring virality on TikTok, several research studies and commercial tools have proposed their own metrics and predictive models \parencite{roring2024decoding, SahJordan2025DecodingReddit}. Moreover, no prior research has applied standard virality criteria to TikTok videos in the context of Nepal’s 2022 elections, necessitating a flexible approach to capture its role in shaping public attention. In this study, virality is operationalized as a composite score emphasizing relative audience engagement, prioritizing interaction over raw popularity.
Four \textbf{normalized engagement rates} - like, comment, share, repost, were calculated.

The rates were combined with the logarithm of raw view count to form a \textbf{virality score}, using a weighted formula that emphasizes active user engagement. The final formula is:
\begin{multline}
\text{virality score} = 0.35 \cdot \log(1 + \text{views}) + 0.25 \cdot \log(1 + \text{like rate}) + 0.20 \cdot \\ \log(1 + \text{comment rate}) + 0.15 \cdot \log(1 + \text{share rate}) \\ + 0.05 \cdot \log(1 + \text{collect rate})
\end{multline}
Weightings of (0.35, 0.25, 0.20, 0.15, 0.05) were assigned to prioritize active engagement behaviors. This operationalization is necessarily imperfect: the inclusion of views risks conflating engagement intensity with baseline exposure, while any fixed weighting scheme remains partly researcher-imposed. To address this concern, RQ1 supplements the legacy score with alternative, less view-dependent target definitions as robustness checks. Given the exploratory nature of this study and the absence of an established benchmark for Nepal’s TikTok context, the composite score should be understood as an analytically useful proxy for political attention rather than as a definitive measure of virality.
The score was transformed into a ternary classification label for RQ1:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Low Virality (0)}: Bottom third of scores.
    \item \textbf{Moderate Virality (1)}: Middle third.
    \item \textbf{High Virality (2)}: Top third.
\end{itemize}
For RQ2, a log-transformed version of the virality score was used as a continuous regression target to account for its skewed distribution.
\newpage
\section{Multimodal Feature Extraction}

This study extracted multi-modal features from a stratified sample of 2,964 TikTok videos to predict virality. Features were derived from three primary modalities: audio transcripts, video descriptions, and visual frames.

\subsection{Textual Features}
Textual or Content features aim to capture the semantic, emotional, and contextual characteristics of a video’s actual media content. These include both linguistic and visual elements:

\subsubsection{Audio Transcripts}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Transcribed using OpenAI’s GPT-4o Whisper API \parencite{OpenAI2024gpt4o}, primarily in Nepali with minor instances of Hindi, Bhojpuri/Maithili, Urdu, and English. Manual corrections added contextual annotations (e.g., \texttt{\{\{Party\_Name\}\} Song}). Transcripts were embedded using the BGE-m3 model \parencite{bge-m3} and reduced via PCA (\texttt{audio\_pca\_0} to \texttt{audio\_pca\_99}).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Video Descriptions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Video Descriptions}: Language detection via LangDetect \parencite{langdetect} identified 494 English and 2,470 non-English (Romanized Nepali-English, Devanagari) descriptions. Non-English descriptions were translated to English using GPT-4o \parencite{OpenAI2024gpt4o}, preserving hashtags and mentions. Descriptions were embedded with BGE-m3 and reduced via PCA (\texttt{desc\_pca\_*}).

    \begin{verbatim}
    prompt = f"Translate the following text to English but keep all 
    hashtags and mentions (like #nepal, @username) 
    unchanged:\n\n{masked_text}"
    \end{verbatim}
\end{itemize}
\newpage
\subsection{Visual Features}
\begin{itemize}
 \item \textbf{Visual Features}: A frame at the 3-second mark was extracted \parencite{TikTokCreative2023}, captioned using GPT-4o Vision API \parencite{OpenAI2024gpt4o}, embedded with BGE-m3, and reduced via PCA (\texttt{image\_pca\_*}).
    \item \textbf{Sentiment Scores}: Extracted for audio, descriptions, and image captions using a pretrained sentiment model.
    \item \textbf{Lexical Features}: Included \texttt{audio\_word\_count}, \texttt{image\_word\_count}, and \texttt{hashtag\_count}.    

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Platform Features}
Platform features refer to platform-mediated affordances and behavioral traces that reflect how users engage with the content:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Temporal Dynamics}: \texttt{video\_hour\_npt}, \texttt{video\_dayofweek\_npt}.
    \item \textbf{Metadata}: \texttt{video\_diversification} labels, duet/stitch indicators.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sender Features}

Sender features capture the video uploader’s social capital and credibility:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Creator Tier Metrics}: A categorical feature, \texttt{creator\_tier}, was derived from follower counts to analyze non-linear effects of audience reach on virality. Using the empirical distribution of follower counts across 2,964 videos (25th percentile: $\approx 983$, 50th: $\approx 2,191$, 75th: $\approx 8,120$, 90th: $\approx 48,740$, 95th: $\approx 108,700$, 99th: $\approx 376,500$), we defined tiers as: Regular ($< 10,000$ followers), Micro Influencer ($10,000–49,999$), Macro Influencer ($50,000–99,999$), and Mega Influencer ($\geq 100,000$). This data-driven approach ensures relevance to Nepal’s 2022 election context and enhances model interpretability.
\newpage
    \item \textbf{Verification Status}: A binary feature, \texttt{author\_verified}, indicates official TikTok verification, reflecting institutional presence or public figure status that may influence platform distribution and audience trust.
    
    \item \textbf{Account Age}: Derived from \texttt{author\_create\_time}, \texttt{author\_days\_on\_platform} measures the creator’s active duration on TikTok, capturing their tenure and experience, which may affect content strategy and reach.
\end{itemize}
\subsection{Feature Integration \& Pre-Processing}

Predictor variables from platform metadata, sender attributes, and content modalities were integrated into a unified, model-ready dataset. Numerical features (e.g., follower count, interaction rates like \texttt{like\_rate}, \texttt{comment\_rate}) were z-score normalized for comparability, while categorical variables (e.g., day-of-week) were one-hot encoded. Multimodal embeddings from audio transcripts, image snapshots, and video descriptions were generated using a multilingual transformer model, reduced via PCA, and used in their compact latent form without further scaling, as they already captured normalized semantic similarity.

To prevent label leakage, post-performance variables (\texttt{video\_like\_count}, \texttt{video\_commentcount}, \texttt{video\_sharecount}, and their normalized counterparts \texttt{like\_rate}, \texttt{comment\_rate}, \texttt{share\_rate}, \texttt{collect\_rate}) were excluded, focusing strictly on pre-upload features like content semantics (text, image, audio) and user metadata (e.g., \texttt{creator\_tier}, verification status). This structured predictor matrix provided a consistent input format and enabled comparison of content, platform, and sender contributions to virality prediction.

\section{Sentence Embedding Model Selection}
We evaluate four sentence embedding models on Nepali election-related texts:
\begin{itemize}
    \item LaBSE \parencite{reimers-2019}
    \item paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2 (MiniLM-L12) \parencite{paraphrase-multilingual-MiniLM-L12-v2}
    \item all-MiniLM-L6-v2 \parencite{all-MiniLM-L6-v2}
    \item BGE-M3 \parencite{bge-m3}
\end{itemize}

BGE-M3 demonstrates multi-lingual capabilities supporting 100+ languages with dense/sparse retrieval \parencite{bge-m3}. The MiniLM variants offer optimized performance for semantic similarity tasks \parencite{reimers-2019}.

Based on evaluation of sentence-embeddings (See Table~\ref{tab:embedding_comparison_appendix}) for 11-pairs of random sentence-text pairs, BGE-m3 was chosen for its more balanced performance in compared to other models.

\section{Analytical Framework}

The analysis addresses the two research questions through two complementary approaches:

\subsection{Machine Learning for Virality Prediction (RQ1)}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Model, Training, and Validation}: RQ1 is estimated using a leakage-safe prediction pipeline that compares sender, content, and platform feature groups across repeated stratified cross-validation folds. The main reported target reduces the weight of raw views relative to engagement rates, while more view-dependent and no-view variants are used as robustness checks. Performance is evaluated using accuracy, macro F1-score, and AUC-ROC.
    
    \item \textbf{Model Interpretation}: Feature-group comparisons and explainability analysis are used to assess whether sender, content, or platform variables contribute most to predictive performance. The aim is not to infer causality, but to identify which observable pre-upload characteristics carry the most predictive information about later virality.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Identification Limits and Causal Feasibility for RQ2}
\label{sec:rq2_identification}

RQ2 asks how communication styles and political content themes are associated
with engagement outcomes on TikTok. A causal interpretation would require a much
stronger design. Communication styles are not randomly assigned; creators choose
them based on who they are, how they position themselves politically, and what
kind of audience they expect to reach.

For that reason, a credible causal comparison would require videos that are
similar on key pre-treatment characteristics but differ in style. To check
whether such a comparison is plausible in this dataset, this study uses
propensity score diagnostics. These scores were estimated for focal styles such
as \textit{Charisma/Leadership} using only pre-treatment sender and timing
covariates, including verification status, follower count, account age, posting
time, and distance from election day.

The results show very limited overlap between videos that use these styles and
videos that do not. In practical terms, the groups are too different from one
another to support a credible ``like-for-like'' comparison. Even after trimming
the sample, videos coded as \textit{Charisma/Leadership} remain structurally
distinct from non-charismatic videos on observable characteristics.

For this reason, the thesis does not interpret RQ2 coefficients as causal
effects of style. Instead, the regression results are treated as conditional
associations: they describe which styles and themes appear alongside higher or
lower engagement after accounting for a limited set of observed controls, but
they do not show that changing style alone would change engagement.
\section{Data Annotation and Validation}
\label{sec:data_annotation}

A systematic annotation and validation process was used to assess the quality and limitations of the multimodal features and labels used for the RQ1 prediction models and the RQ2 regression analysis on 2,964 TikTok videos from Nepal’s 2022 elections.

\subsection{Audio Transcripts}
A 5\% sample (\(n=148\)) of Nepali audio transcripts (\texttt{audio\_transcript\_np}), converted to PCA embeddings (\texttt{audio\_pca\_*}), was manually validated, achieving 87.2\% accuracy by the primary author and a Cohen’s Kappa of 0.462 (moderate agreement) with a secondary evaluator \parencite{landis1977measurement}. Despite dialectal challenges, embeddings were retained due to their complementary role with image (\texttt{image\_pca\_*}) and description (\texttt{desc\_pca\_*}) embeddings, with \texttt{audio\_pca\_0} showing a mean $|$SHAP$|$ $\approx$ 0.3.

\subsection{Video Descriptions}
A 5\% sample (\(n=148\)) of translated video descriptions achieved 98.6\% accuracy, preserving hashtags per best practices \parencite{highfield2016instagrammatics}. These were converted to PCA embeddings (\texttt{desc\_pca\_*}) for the XGBoost model.

\subsection{Image Embeddings}
Image features were derived by captioning a frame with GPT-4o Vision and embedding the resulting caption with BGE-m3 before PCA reduction. These image-derived components (\texttt{image\_pca\_*}) were used directly in the XGBoost model without separate manual validation, with \texttt{image\_pca\_2} showing a mean $|$SHAP$|$ $\approx$ 0.1 for high-virality predictions.

\subsection{Style and Content Labels for RQ2}
Three annotators labeled all 2,964 videos for communication styles (\texttt{style\_1\_label}, e.g., Comedic) and content themes (\texttt{content\_label}, e.g., party affiliation) per a predefined codebook. A 150-video sample yielded a Cohen’s Kappa of 0.622 (substantial agreement) for \texttt{style\_1\_label} \parencite{landis1977measurement}, supporting its use in OLS regression. Content labels, derived via zero-shot classification and manually reviewed, lacked formal validation due to resource constraints.

\subsection{Discussion}
Validation confirms reliability, with 98.6\% accuracy for video descriptions and Kappa = 0.622 for style labels, though audio transcripts’ Kappa = 0.462 reflects linguistic challenges. CLIP embeddings align with computer vision standards, and zero-shot content labels were mitigated by manual review. This ensures dataset suitability for modeling virality and style–theme interactions, though future work could improve audio transcription using advanced multilingual NLP fine-tuned for Nepalese social media communication context.

\section{Ethical Considerations}
Data collection used publicly accessible TikTok content and complied with the platform access methods described in the data section. No personally sensitive information beyond public account-level metadata was intentionally collected or analyzed, and no contact was made with platform users. Because this study relies on observational platform data, the main ethical concerns are measurement error, contextual misinterpretation, and the responsible reporting of public political speech in a low-resource setting. These limitations and potential biases are reported transparently throughout the thesis.

\section{Error Analysis}
An error analysis was conducted on a stratified test sample ($n=890$) to identify misclassification patterns in the tuned XGBoost benchmark model (accuracy: 0.60, AUC-ROC: 0.7871) for ternary virality classification (0 = low, 1 = medium, 2 = high). Three specific cases and a general pattern of class boundary errors were examined to understand the model’s behavior.

\textbf{Case 1: Overprediction of Medium Virality as High}
A medium-viral video (ID: 7095593756967046426, true label: 1) with 2,501 views, 232 likes, 3,161 followers, a creator \texttt{user\_like\_count} of 19,854, and \texttt{Video\_count} of 155 was mispredicted as high-viral (Class 2). The model’s reliance on \texttt{user\_like\_count} (mean $|$SHAP$|$ $\approx$ 0.8) and \texttt{Video\_count} (mean $|$SHAP$|$ $\approx$ 0.5), which strongly influence high-virality predictions, likely drove this overprediction, as the creator’s high engagement history overshadowed the video’s moderate engagement metrics.

\textbf{Case 2: Underprediction of High Virality as Medium}
A high-viral video (ID: 7089029999780498715, true label: 2) with 2,820,140 views, 161,239 likes, 2,382 comments, 2,423 shares, 49,400 followers, a \texttt{user\_like\_count} of 1,300,000, and \texttt{Video\_count} of 1,876 was mispredicted as medium-viral (Class 1). Despite exceptionally high engagement metrics, the prediction may have been moderated by content features favoring medium virality (e.g., \texttt{audio\_pca\_0}, mean $|$SHAP$|$ $\approx$ 0.3). Although \texttt{user\_like\_count} and \texttt{Video\_count} strongly support high-virality predictions, the model may struggle to capture the combined effect of extreme engagement values, possibly due to the limited impact of content embeddings (mean SHAP: audio 0.0013, image 0.0010).

\textbf{Case 3: Misclassifications Between Adjacent Classes}
Misclassifications were most frequent between adjacent classes, particularly low (Class 0) and medium (Class 1) virality, with 62\% of Class 1 errors misclassified as Class 0 and 48\% of Class 0 errors as Class 1, highlighting the model’s challenge in distinguishing nuanced engagement levels, as evidenced by the low F1-score for Class 1 (0.51).

\section{Communication Styles and Political Content Themes}
To examine how communication styles and political content themes relate to virality on TikTok during Nepal’s local elections, we first estimate Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) models as a descriptive baseline and then conduct explicit
causal feasibility diagnostics—including within-sender assessment and propensity score analysis—to evaluate whether causal interpretation is supported by the data. The main RQ2 specification is a reduced additive OLS model with communication-style indicators, political content-theme indicators, and controls for log-transformed follower count, author verification status, and days before election day. A fully interacted style--theme model is retained only as a sensitivity analysis because many interaction cells are sparse and the resulting design matrix is unstable. Reduced-model coefficients are reported in Appendix~\ref{tab:ols_virality_main}; the full interaction specification is reported in Appendix~\ref{tab:ols_virality_summary} and Appendix~\ref{tab:ols_virality_interactions}.

\subsection{Model Performance}
The reduced additive model explained approximately 19.5\% of the variance in virality (\( R^2 = 0.195 \), Adjusted \( R^2 = 0.187 \)), a reasonable fit given the high behavioral variability in social media data. The model was statistically significant (\( F = 24.39 \), \( p < 5.29e-61 \)) and numerically more stable than the fully interacted alternative. After excluding four videos with invalid style code values, the reduced model uses 1,526 observations and has a condition number of approximately 206. The fully interacted sensitivity model can also be estimated on the cleaned data, but it has a notably higher condition number (approximately 650), higher variance inflation for several terms, and heavier reliance on sparse cells. For this reason, the additive specification is treated as the main descriptive model for RQ2.

\subsection{Dataset and Category Inclusion}
The filtered dataset from the sample comprised 1,530 TikTok videos, with political content identified through keyword and theme-based labeling. Five political content categories were included in the analysis, also their name along with their election symbol in Nepali script:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item Independent candidates (e.g., Balen Shah \texthindi{लौरो/स्वतन्त्र}) — count: 232
    \item CPN (UML) (\texthindi{सूर्य/एमाले}) — count: 271
    \item Nepali Congress (\texthindi{रुख/काँग्रेस}) — count: 256
    \item CPN (Maoist) (\texthindi{हँसिया हतौडा/माओवादी}) — count: 84
    \item General election-themed content (non-party affiliated) — count: 687
\end{itemize}
\newpage
Of the 2,964 sampled videos, 1,530 were retained after restricting the analysis to the five political content categories. First, videos assigned to non-political content themes via zero-shot classification---including Bhojpuri/Maithili songs ($n \approx 410$), humorous or satirical content not tied to specific parties ($n \approx 320$), patriotic songs ($n \approx 85$), and unclassified speech or explanatory content ($n \approx 619$)---were excluded to maintain thematic focus on identifiable political actors and address severe class imbalance that would otherwise distort coefficient estimates. These non-political videos were retained separately for exploratory style distribution analysis to contextualize broader communication strategies. Second, videos whose content theme could not be reliably assigned through keyword and theme-based labeling were excluded. For the main additive OLS model, four additional videos with invalid style code values were dropped, leaving 1,526 observations. Certain style--theme combinations, such as Comedic--Maoist (\( n = 5 \)), remain very sparse and motivate treating the fully interacted model only as a sensitivity analysis.

\subsection{Main Effects}
The reduced additive regression (see Appendix~\ref{tab:ols_virality_main}) revealed significant main effects for content themes and control variables. Coefficient tests are reported with HC3 robust standard errors:
\begin{itemize}[noitemsep]
    \item \textbf{Independent candidate content} was positively associated with virality compared to the baseline election-themed content ($\beta = 0.3046$, $p < 0.001$), reflecting strong engagement even after accounting for follower reach and posting timing.
    \item \textbf{Communication styles}: Relative to the reference category of \textit{Civic Awareness}, \textit{Charisma/Leadership} ($\beta = 0.3867$, $p = 0.003$) and \textit{Critique/Frustration} ($\beta = 0.3577$, $p = 0.012$) were positively associated with virality in the additive model. These coefficients should be interpreted cautiously, however, because the within-creator and overlap diagnostics indicate that style is highly endogenous to specific actors and contexts.
    \item \textbf{Control variables}: Larger follower counts ($\beta_{\text{log(followers)}} = 1.0313$, $p < 0.001$) and posting closer to election day ($\beta = 0.0058$, $p = 0.016$) were positively associated with virality. The coefficient on verified creators was negative ($\beta = -0.8951$, $p = 0.005$), but this term should be interpreted cautiously because verified accounts are very rare in the dataset.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Interaction Effects}
Interaction coefficients are reported only as a sensitivity analysis in the appendix. Even after cleaning the coding and fixing the reference categories, the fully interacted style--theme model remains less stable than the additive specification because several combinations are sparse and some terms exhibit elevated collinearity. Accordingly, no interaction coefficient is treated as a core RQ2 finding. The more reliable conclusion is that main effects---especially independent-candidate content and follower reach, and more cautiously additive style differences relative to Civic Awareness---account for more of the observed variation than any single style--theme interaction.

    \item \textbf{Non-political content} paired with folk, instrumental, or romantic Nepali pop music, emphasizing cultural appeal.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Discussion}
\label{chap:discussion}

This study asks two simple questions about Nepal’s 2022 local elections on TikTok: can we predict which political videos will attract attention, and are some communication styles or political themes linked to higher engagement than others? The answer is mixed. Some patterns are clear, but the data also place real limits on what can be claimed.

The central empirical pattern is straightforward. Virality is shaped more by \textit{who} is posting than by content features alone. In other words, political attention on TikTok in Nepal is not only about message design. It is also about pre-existing visibility, audience size, and who is already well positioned to be seen.

\section{Predictability of Political Virality (RQ1)}
\label{sec:rq1_discussion}
The refactored RQ1 pipeline shows that political virality is moderately predictable from pre-upload features, but that prediction is driven primarily by sender characteristics. Under the primary, less view-dependent target, the best full model achieves an AUC-ROC of approximately 0.80, while a sender-only model performs nearly as well. By contrast, content-only and platform-only models perform substantially worse. This pattern is more important than the exact headline metric: it indicates that baseline creator capacity carries far more predictive information than multimodal content features on their own.

For a non-technical reader, the meaning of this result is simple: the model can tell something useful about future visibility, but it mostly does so by learning which accounts were already strong to begin with. This also helps explain why medium virality remains difficult to classify. Videos in the middle of the distribution do not look as clearly different from one another as the lowest- and highest-performing videos.

\subsection{Sender Capacity}

Sender characteristics, particularly prior creator engagement, posting history, and follower count, dominate the predictive models. Substantively, this suggests that political virality on TikTok in Nepal is not well explained by content features alone. Videos are embedded in pre-existing creator trajectories, so account-level visibility and accumulated audience attention matter before a specific video is even evaluated.

At the same time, this result should not be over-read as proof of TikTok’s hidden algorithm. What the data show is simpler and safer: observed political visibility is unequally distributed. That inequality may reflect some combination of platform recommendation, existing follower base, cross-platform promotion, and offline public prominence.

\subsection{Limits of Predictive Modeling}
The model is useful, but it is not magic. It captures some broad patterns well, especially around creator scale, but it is weaker at capturing context-specific meaning. That matters in Nepal, where language mixing, regional dialects, humor, and local political symbolism are often central to how content is understood. Frequent confusion between low and medium virality reflects this limitation: not every politically important difference is easy to represent as a machine-readable feature.
\newpage

\section{Revisiting RQ2: What Can (and Cannot) Be Claimed}
This study also examined how communication styles and political content themes are associated with virality on TikTok. Here the key point is caution: the analysis does not support a causal interpretation of style coefficients. 

Diagnostic analyses—including within-sender assessment and propensity score–based overlap checks—show that communication styles such as \textit{Charisma/Leadership} or \textit{Comedic} content are highly selective and lack sufficient counterfactual overlap. Consequently:
\begin{itemize}
    \item These styles cannot be treated as manipulable treatments.
    \item Independent causal effects on engagement cannot be reliably estimated.
    \item Results are interpreted as \textbf{conditional associations} describing the observed political communication ecosystem.
\end{itemize}

\section{Communication Style as a Structural Signal}
A central insight is that communication style is difficult to interpret as an independent driver of virality. In the reduced additive model, \textit{Charisma/Leadership} and \textit{Critique/Frustration} are positively associated with virality relative to \textit{Civic Awareness}. However, these associations become harder to sustain as standalone substantive claims once richer interaction structures, overlap diagnostics, and within-creator sensitivity checks are considered. The within-creator analysis in particular shows little evidence that switching style within the same creator is associated with large systematic changes in virality once creator-level differences are absorbed.

In simpler terms, style seems to travel with the speaker. It is not just a tool that any political actor can pick up and use with the same effect. For example, \textit{Charisma/Leadership} is disproportionately associated with independent candidates (primarily associated with Balen Shah) and nearly absent among legacy-party accounts. Its apparent effectiveness depends on:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Political legitimacy:} Who is allowed to speak in a certain way.
    \item \textbf{Prior visibility:} The baseline reach of the creator.
    \item \textbf{Audience trust:} The perceived authenticity of the performance.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Independent Candidates: Virality and Authenticity}
Content related to independent candidate (mainly Balen Shah) followed by Harka Sampang, and Gopi Hamal exhibits substantially higher engagement than legacy-party content. Independent candidate videos achieve the highest mean virality scores, even when controlling for follower counts.

Charismatic performance—expressed through rap, hip-hop, and symbols like the \textit{lauro}—is a defining characteristic of prominent independent-candidate content in the dataset. The evidence is strongest for the descriptive claim that this style clusters around independent actors and accompanies higher engagement in that subset, not for the stronger claim that charisma itself independently raises virality across actors.

The more defensible interpretation is therefore relational rather than tactical. Independent-candidate content appears to combine a favorable political moment, recognizable symbols, and creator-specific authenticity in ways that align with higher engagement. In a low-income but highly connected country like Nepal, this matters because social media can help new political actors bypass parts of the traditional media system. But the findings also suggest that this opening is not equal for everyone; visibility still depends heavily on who already has reach and recognition.


\section{Legacy Parties and Conditional Resonance}
Legacy parties (UML, Congress, Maoists) generally show lower average engagement. However, the interaction between \textit{Critique/Frustration} and UML-related content is positively associated with virality.

\section{Integrating RQ2 with RQ1}
While the XGBoost model in RQ1 shows that virality is predictable, it also reveals that \textbf{sender-related features} (follower count, prior engagement) dominate performance. RQ2 clarifies this: 
\begin{quote}
RQ1 establishes that virality is predictable largely due to \textit{who} is speaking, while RQ2 shows that \textit{how} they speak is constrained by \textit{who} they are.
\end{quote}

\section{Broader Implications}

The findings carry implications across four audiences: researchers, policymakers and election authorities, citizens and civil society, and political practitioners.

\textbf{For researchers}, this study demonstrates that observational social media data cannot sustain strong causal claims about communication strategy effects without explicit identification testing. The propensity score diagnostics presented here---rarely conducted in political TikTok research---should become standard practice before attributing engagement differences to content choices. The open-source multimodal pipeline developed here is directly transferable to comparable Global South contexts where such analytical infrastructure is absent.

\textbf{For policymakers and election authorities}, the dominance of sender features in predicting virality suggests that political visibility on TikTok is structured by unequal creator capacity, not just by the content of individual videos. In Nepal, this matters because short-video platforms can give citizens low-cost access to campaign information, political commentary, satire, and candidates who may receive less attention in legacy media. That is a real democratic opportunity. But it also creates an uneven visibility landscape in which candidates with pre-existing digital audiences may compound their reach, whether through platform recommendation, cross-platform spillover, or offline prominence. Nepal banned TikTok in 2023 and reinstated it in 2024, with debates driven largely by misinformation concerns \parencite{lamichhane2024tik, reuters2024tiktok}. The present findings cannot identify TikTok's internal ranking rules directly, but they still point to clear practical priorities for Nepal.

First, the \textbf{Election Commission of Nepal} should ask platforms for more transparency during election periods. In plain terms, this means not only asking whether harmful content is being removed, but also asking which political accounts, influencers, and campaign-related videos are receiving especially high visibility.

Second, Nepal’s election authorities, public-interest researchers, and the government bodies responsible for digital policy should push for \textbf{better archival and research access}. One of the biggest limits of this thesis was that election-period platform data were incomplete and hard to verify. Without a better archive of public political content and metadata, independent scrutiny after an election remains weak.

Third, campaign oversight in Nepal should treat \textbf{digital reach as a campaign resource}. If some actors enter the campaign with much larger online audiences, then visibility is not evenly distributed from the start. This suggests a need for closer scrutiny of high-reach political amplification, including influencer promotion, coordinated visibility campaigns, and the role of large intermediary accounts.

Finally, the findings support \textbf{public media literacy efforts} in Nepal that help citizens read online popularity more carefully. A highly viewed or highly shared political video should not automatically be taken as a signal of broad public support; it may also reflect the advantage of already prominent accounts.

\textbf{For citizens and civil society}, the core message is plain: social media can be useful during an election because it helps people see speeches, campaign clips, criticism, humor, and local political discussion quickly and at low cost. This is especially important in a country like Nepal, where digital platforms may be easier to access than many traditional sources of political information. But what becomes visible online is not necessarily what most people support. It is partly shaped by who already has digital reach. For journalists, watchdog groups, and ordinary voters, this means viral political content should be read with one more question in mind: who is being amplified, and who may be left out of view?

\textbf{For political practitioners}, the main practical implication is negative rather than prescriptive: the analysis does not justify strong claims about ``what works'' stylistically on TikTok. The evidence is more consistent with the view that communication styles operate as structural signals tied to specific actors and audiences. Legacy parties therefore cannot assume that copying the visible form of independent-candidate content will reproduce its engagement outcomes.


\chapter{Conclusion}

This study examined TikTok's role in shaping political attention during Nepal's 2022 Local Elections through two complementary research questions: predicting political virality using pre-upload features (RQ1), and examining how communication styles and political content themes are associated with engagement outcomes (RQ2). Situated in a low-income, multilingual, but highly digital context, the study contributes to computational political communication research while also speaking to a wider public debate about what social media means for elections in Nepal.

\textbf{On RQ1}, the refactored prediction pipeline shows that political virality on TikTok can be predicted with moderate success from pre-upload features, but that sender-related characteristics---prior engagement history and follower count---consistently matter more than content or platform features alone. The key conclusion is therefore about structural visibility: political attention on TikTok is not well explained as a purely content-driven process, because creator capacity carries substantial predictive information.

\textbf{On RQ2}, regression analysis revealed that content associated with independent candidates achieved substantially higher engagement than either general election-themed or legacy-party content---the highest mean virality score in the dataset ($z_{bc_{\text{virality}}} = 0.4949$), even after controlling for follower reach. By contrast, communication-style coefficients are unstable across model specifications and diagnostic checks. The safest interpretation is therefore that styles such as \textit{Charisma/Leadership} are concentrated in independent-candidate content and function as actor-bound signals rather than as independently validated drivers of engagement.

\textbf{Policy relevance.} These findings speak directly to governance challenges that Nepal faced when it banned TikTok in 2023 and reversed course in 2024 \parencite{lamichhane2024tik, reuters2024tiktok}. Short-video platforms can widen access to political information and help newer actors reach voters, which is especially important in a low-income but highly connected country such as Nepal. But content moderation and platform bans address only one dimension of the problem. The deeper challenge suggested by this thesis is unequal political visibility on short-video platforms, which may reflect the interaction of creator scale, engagement dynamics, and platform design. The main policy lesson is therefore institutional rather than stylistic. For Nepal, this means that the Election Commission, digital-policy authorities, and public-interest researchers need better transparency about how political content is distributed, better archival access to election-period platform data, and campaign oversight rules that recognize digital reach as a form of political advantage. In simpler terms: the problem is not only bad content, but also unequal visibility. For citizens, the practical meaning is equally simple: a viral political message may be influential, but it is not automatically representative.

\textbf{Methodological contribution.} By explicitly testing and rejecting causal identification assumptions rather than silently reporting OLS coefficients as effects, this analysis provides a replicable template for political communication research in contexts where experimental or quasi-experimental designs are unavailable. The open-source multimodal pipeline, composite virality index, and communication style codebook are transferable to comparable settings. Future work should pursue longitudinal designs that track the same creators across election cycles, cross-platform comparisons that isolate TikTok-specific effects from broader social media dynamics, and---as API access matures---network-level analyses of repost and follower graph structures that the current TikTok Research API does not yet support \parencite{tiktokResearchAPI2025}.

\section{Limitations}
\label{limitation}
While the baseline single-split benchmark achieved about 60\% accuracy and the refactored pipeline showed moderate predictive performance across validation settings, several limitations warrant acknowledgment:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Predictor Variables and Sampling}: Time constraints limited processing of the full 28,000-video dataset. Stratified sampling ensured coverage across the engagement distribution, but the subset’s representativeness relative to the full corpus was not fully validated. Because the sample was initially drawn using view-based strata, some degree of selection toward already visible content cannot be ruled out.

    \item \textbf{Audio Transcripts}: 
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Open AI’s Whisper model struggled with audio mixed with sound effects, retaining only clear segments due to time limitations.
            \item Manual labeling of Nepali text for contextual meanings (e.g., ``party song,'' ``humor'') was applied, but transcripts in other languages (e.g., Urdu, Chinese), slang, or noisy audio posed challenges. Contextual labels (e.g., ``Newari Language,'' ``Bhojpuri/Maithili song'') lacked validation.
            \item Nepal’s linguistic diversity led to language estimation based on the author’s knowledge, reducing transcript accuracy.
            \item Differing audio-video tones (e.g., fun audio vs.\ satirical video) confused RQ2 labeling.
            \item A Cohen’s Kappa score of 0.462 indicates moderate agreement for transcripts \parencite{datatab2025, kolena2025}, reflecting challenges for LLMs in processing low-resource languages like Nepali’s dialects and noisy social media content \parencite{icuc2025, leanmean2023}.
        \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Video Descriptions}:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Semantic interpretation of Nepali humor, dialects, and mixed Romanized English-Nepali text was challenging. Names like \texthindi{सागर} (Sagar, mistranslated as ``Ocean'') or \texthindi{बसन्ते} (Basante, ``Spring'') in campaign songs required manual correction to capture satirical intent.
            \item Accuracy was assessed on a 5\% sample, potentially unrepresentative of the full dataset.
            \item Empty descriptions and Lang Detect API errors misidentifying mixed text as English skipped translations, affecting embedding accuracy.
        \end{itemize}
\newpage
    \item \textbf{Image Analysis}:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Image analysis missed contextual nuances (e.g., running mistaken for walking in screenshots).
            \item Using 3-second clips, inspired by TikTok marketing, lacked academic validation in this context.
        \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Style\_1\_Label}:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item The custom codebook struggled with blended social media content (e.g., humor, music, vlogging), limiting nuanced meaning capture.
            \item Annotator bias led to subjective categorizations (e.g., rally videos as ``communal'' [Category 4], ``event coverage'' [Category 9], or ``personal vlogging'' [Category 3]). High Cohen’s Kappa ($>0.60$) was sample-based and not standardized for Nepali election contexts.
            \item Communal and event coverage labels were often conflated, as communal themes appeared in documentary-style or rally footage.
            \item Discrepancies between the author’s codebook and annotators were resolved only for significant differences, risking minor inconsistencies.
        \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Content\_Theme}:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Zero-shot labeling struggled with humorous multi-party support videos, forcing single-theme selection.
            \item Symbolic imagery (e.g., money in a child’s mouth) was insufficient for clear thematic categorization.
        \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Within-Creator Variation for RQ2}:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Although a subset of creators posted videos in multiple styles, within-creator variation remained limited relative to the full political sample. This constrains the extent to which communication styles can be compared net of stable creator characteristics.
            \item As a result, RQ2 is better suited to describing actor-linked style patterns than to isolating the independent contribution of style alone.
        \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Platform Features}:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item The \texttt{video\_diversification\_id} (e.g., \texttt{id\_10083.0}) had sparse, missing API labels, complicating content type interpretation (Table~\ref{tab:diversification_label}). Overfitting to dominant IDs (e.g., \texttt{video\_diversification\_id\_0.0}) may reduce generalizability for impactful, less frequent IDs influencing high virality (Class 2).
        \end{itemize}
\newpage
    \item \textbf{Transformer Models for Low-Resource Settings}:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item Open-source models like Wiseyak \parencite{duwal2024domainadaptativecontinuallearninglowresource} and NepaliBERT \parencite{Pudasaini2023NepaliBERT} were limited to Devanagari text, and still surprisingly underperformed on Devanagiri setting, let alone mixed Romanized Nepali-English content. SeamlessM4T \parencite{elbayad2023seamlessm4t, facebookresearch2023seamlessm4t, huggingface2024seamlessm4t} excelled in transliteration and translation than Nepalese fine-tuned counterpart (for Devanagiri). Comparative analysis among these models were not acknowledged due to time constraints.
            \item No Hugging Face model fully addressed transliteration, translation, and semantic embedding for code-switched Nepali-English text. SeamlessM4T-medium handled Devanagari better, but struggled with code-switching, while OpenAI’s API proved more accurate \parencite{reddit2023gpt4nepali}.
        \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Data Limitations}:

    The TikTok Research API, while enabling access to public data, suffers from archiving delays and incomplete historical records, limiting accurate view and engagement counts for Nepal’s 2022 election videos \parencite{tiktokResearchAPI2025}. As a relatively new API, it offers room for improvement in data reliability and historical access. Additionally, unofficial APIs \parencite{QBukold2025TikTokScraper}, reliant on web-inspect elements, may have some inaccuracies, as manual inspections revealed discrepancies in variables like \texttt{video\_is\_ai\_gc}, where it is null in metadata, but exists in the actual video.
    
\end{itemize}


\newpage
\section{Future Research}
    Future research could address these limitations by:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Scaling up to the full 28,165-video dataset would enable finer-grained virality levels and more nuanced category distinctions.
        \item Developing time-series models to capture evolving virality dynamics across campaign cycles.
        \item Comparing virality patterns across platforms (e.g., Facebook(Meta) platform, YouTube Shorts, Instagram Reels) to assess platform-specific effects. Facebook still continues to be the most dominant social media platform in Nepal, used by half of country's population \parencite{napoleoncat2025}.
        \item Enhancing comment analysis, including sentiment, to better understand virality impact despite time constraints on initial collection.
        \item Employing fine-tuned multilingual embeddings for Nepali and Romanized text to enhance semantic capture.
        \item Using network analysis to explore virality spread, requiring follower graphs and repost chains. While the current TikTok API provides follower and repost counts, it does not include lists of followers or reposters (likely due to privacy restrictions, despite followers being manually viewable), necessitating future API enhancements to enable such analysis.
        \item Exploring hybrid models with attention mechanisms to improve medium virality prediction.
    \end{itemize}

    By addressing these gaps, researchers can further elucidate the role of popular social media platforms such as TikTok in shaping political communication, particularly in diverse, multilingual contexts like Nepal.

\subsection*{A.5 Development of TikTok Communication Style Codebook}
\label{app:codebook}

This appendix outlines the development and structure of the communication style codebook used to categorize TikTok videos for RQ2 in the analysis of political communication during Nepal’s 2022 Local Elections. The codebook was iteratively refined to capture the nuanced and context-specific nature of TikTok’s political discourse in Nepal, building on established frameworks and empirical observations.

\textbf{Development Process}
The initial codebook was adapted from the communication style classification framework proposed by Umansky and Pipal \parencite{umansky2023dances}, which analyzed political communication on TikTok and included categories such as Comedic, Documentary, Communal, Explanatory, Interactive, Meta, Other, and Not Codeable. This framework provided a theoretical foundation but required adaptation to address the unique characteristics of election-related content in Nepal.

Through an iterative annotation process involving a stratified sample of TikTok videos, the codebook was expanded to incorporate additional styles that emerged as salient in the dataset. These included Musical/Entertainment, Charisma/Leadership Portrayal, Civic Engagement/Awareness, and Populist Critique/Frustration. The refinement process aimed to balance theoretical grounding with inductive insights, ensuring the codebook captured the richness of multimodal political content while remaining practical for annotation. In cases of overlapping styles, annotators prioritized the dominant communicative intent, determined by integrating visual, auditory, and textual cues, as perceived by a typical viewer.

The final codebook, presented in Table~\ref{tab:codebook}, reflects both the adaptation of prior research and context-specific adjustments derived from direct engagement with Nepal’s TikTok political landscape.

\textbf{Codebook Structure}
Table~\ref{tab:codebook} details the communication style codes, their labels, definitions, and illustrative clues observed in video content or transcripts. Each style is designed to encapsulate a distinct mode of political communication, facilitating the analysis of style--theme interactions in the OLS regression model (Appendix~\ref{tab:ols_virality_summary}).
